The problem

The bureaucracy isn't
a bug. It's the architecture.

Universities today run on people acting as the workflow engine. A reimbursement moves through five inboxes before it's approved. An event requires sign-offs from three departments who have never spoken to each other. A student query bounces between admin staff for days.

This isn't a failure of effort. It's a structural problem, one that hardens into bureaucracy as institutions scale. Coordination becomes the job itself.

The result: a bureaucratic operating model that no amount of new software has been able to fix, because the problem isn't the tools. It's the layer between them.

  • Coordination becomes a full-time job at scale
  • Entire teams exist to move work between people who do it
  • Institutional knowledge is lost between handoffs
  • Decisions are delayed by missing context
  • Staff capacity absorbed by follow-ups, not actual work
  • Existing software stores data, nobody executes it
What we're building

A system of execution,
not record.

Most university software is built to store information. Sheldon is built to act on it.

A context-aware operational layer that sits across departments, understanding requests, maintaining institutional memory, and closing the loop without human intermediaries.

Not another ERP. Not another dashboard. Not another ticketing tool.

  • Understands requests across departments
  • Maintains institutional context across workflows
  • Routes tasks dynamically to the right people
  • Automates approvals and follow-ups
  • Tracks workflow state in real time
  • Improves from historical workflow data
